Product Management Case Study

How I'd take a solar water heater from idea to launch

A full product management walkthrough for Solar-R-Us, built end to end: market problem, concept, vision, user stories, beta testing, and demand generation.

⚠️ This case study is based on a fictional company, Solar-R-Us (SRU), used as the scenario for a Coursera Product Management Capstone Project. The company and product are not real. The work product, structure, and thinking are mine.
01 / Intro

What this case study is

A breakdown of the full product management lifecycle, applied to one product, start to almost-finish.

In my own words I wanted a project where I touch every phase a PM actually deals with, not just the fun parts. Concept, requirements, beta testing, going to market, even retiring an old product. So I picked this capstone scenario and built it out properly, like I would for a real product.

The product is a commercial solar water heating system. The company is Solar-R-Us. Through the case I worked as the product manager, taking the idea from a rough market opportunity all the way through a beta test and into a demand generation plan for launch.

Below you'll find the actual documents I produced along the way. Some are summarized here, the full versions are linked at the bottom of each section.

02 / The Company

About Solar-R-Us (SRU)

📌 Disclaimer: Solar-R-Us LLC is a fictional company created for the Coursera Product Management Specialization capstone project. All scenario details, financials, and customer information below come from the course materials, not a real business.
Who they are

A solar products company based in the U.S.

SRU produces a range of solar-powered products: water heating systems, pool heating systems, and solar powered heaters, for both commercial and residential use. They sell direct through their website and through retail partners like Amazon, Home Depot, Lowe's, Walmart, and Costco.

The problem they're solving

Energy costs are going up, and the world wants cleaner heat

Conventional water heating is getting more expensive and less acceptable as regulations tighten. SRU's bet is a system that's cheaper to run, easier to manage, and friendlier to the environment than what's already out there.

07 / Beta Testing

In my own words What I liked about this part of the case is it wasn't perfect. The large system had real issues, the app didn't recognize the configuration, the collector efficiency was lower than planned. That's normal. A beta test that finds nothing wrong probably wasn't testing hard enough.

What the test actually found

  • Small and moderate systems worked exactly as planned.
  • Large system had a collector efficiency issue, engineering committed to a fix before launch.
  • The app didn't recognize the large system configuration, fix was expedited.
  • Users wanted a downloadable illustrated manual instead of an all-text guide.
  • Overall, users were satisfied and very interested in cost comparisons against competitors.

09 / Reflection

What I'd take from this

In my own words The biggest thing this case reinforced for me is that product management isn't one skill, it's switching hats constantly. Concept work is almost philosophical, you're arguing about market timing and positioning. Beta testing is operational, you're watching real people get stuck on a screen. Demand gen is almost a different job entirely. A good PM has to be comfortable being a beginner in each of those modes, over and over, on the same product.
In my own words I also caught myself a few times assuming the PM owns more than they actually do. Marketing owns the campaign. Manufacturing owns packaging and logistics readiness. My job was connective tissue, making sure those teams had what they needed from the product side, not running their work for them.
